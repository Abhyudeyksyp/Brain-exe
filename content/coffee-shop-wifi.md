# Coffee Shop Wifi Tiers: A Scientific Classification

As someone who has made a remote work lifestyle out of coffee shops, I've developed a complex understanding of their hierarchy. It's not about the coffee (though that matters). It's not about the aesthetic (though Instagram thinks it is). It's about one crucial variable: WiFi stability.

## Tier S: The Hallowed Halls

**Characteristics:**
- 5GHz band available
- Upload speeds faster than 10 Mbps
- The WiFi name is professional and password-less
- Bandwidth mysteriously doesn't degrade during peak hours
- Laptop users are respected and given the best tables

**Example:** That one local place that nobody talks about but everyone who knows it does remote work there.

**Reality:** These don't actually exist. This is mythology.

## Tier A: The Reliable Standard

**Characteristics:**
- 2.4GHz band actually works most of the time
- Drops less than 20 minutes per 8-hour work session
- Password is the owner's name or business name
- Staff brings you water without asking
- Noise level appropriate for concentration

**Reality:** You've probably been to one once and will never find it again.

## Tier B: The Functional Compromise

**Characteristics:**
- WiFi works about 70% of the time
- Speeds adequate for browsing and email
- Password is something quirky ("password123" or "wifiwifi")
- Occasional disconnect requires full router restart
- You need to ask for WiFi password every visit even though it's written on a chalk board

**Where they are:** Most of downtown in any major city.

**Soundtrack:** Acoustic guitar + espresso machine + uncomfortable chairs.

## Tier C: The "Let's Try Here"

**Characteristics:**
- WiFi appears in network list, mostly
- Speeds measured in feelings rather than Mbps
- Disconnects mysteriously when you're typing something important
- Password is 47 characters long
- Barista forgot WiFi password exists

**Typical interaction:**
- "Your WiFi isn't working"
- "Did you try turning it off and on?"
- "..."
- *leaves to find different coffee shop*

## Tier D: The Masochist's Choice

**Characteristics:**
- "Sorry, we're having WiFi issues"
- The router is a NetGear from 2009
- Password is handwritten on a napkin from 2019
- Staff is defensive about WiFi quality
- You connect to the neighbor's more reliable network instead

**Why you go:** The coffee is genuinely excellent. Suffering has purpose.

## Tier F: The Bait and Switch

**Characteristics:**
- "Free WiFi!" sign is the most reliable thing there
- WiFi only works if you sit facing the router
- Requires watching ads before connecting
- Disconnects more often than it connects
- Staff: "Yeah, we know. The company that installed it is terrible."

**Reality Check:** At this point, just use your phone's hotspot.

## The Outlier: The Food Truck with Better Connectivity

Every city has that mysterious food truck or pop-up that has inexplicably perfect WiFi. You'll never be able to find it twice. It's a cryptid of the digital nomad world.

## The Real Tier List

Honest assessment of what matters:
1. **WiFi stability** (40%)
2. **Noise level** (25%)
3. **Table comfort and space** (20%)
4. **Coffee quality** (10%)
5. **Aesthetic** (5%)

Nobody wants to admit that the beautiful minimalist coffee shop with Edison bulbs has terrible WiFi. But they do. They always do.

## Life Hack

The best WiFi at coffee shops is usually:
- When you arrive right at open (before peak hours)
- On weekday mornings
- At independent shops rather than chains
- When you use 5GHz if available

The worst WiFi is:
- Peak hours (11am-2pm, 4pm-7pm)
- Weekends
- Places with terrible thermal management (so the router overheats)

## Final Thoughts

Coffee shop WiFi quality is a direct correlate with the coffee shop's success. Successful places are busy, which taxes the bandwidth. Quiet places often have perfect WiFi because nobody's using it.

This is a metaphor for something profound, I'm sure.

Anyway, if you know a Tier A coffee shop, don't tell anyone. Just go there. Protect it. That place is precious.
