# The Death of the Open Web: How Platforms Became Gatekeepers

There was a time when the internet felt different. Smaller. More egalitarian. If you had an idea and basic HTML knowledge, you could build it and put it on the web. Discovery happened through links, search, and communities. The web was a place you visited, not something that visited you.

That era is ending.

## The Great Enclosure

The early 2000s web was fragmented but democratic. Blogs, forums, personal websites—each with their own aesthetic and voice. The rise of platforms changed this fundamentally.

First came MySpace and Friendster (rapid, relative to the tech world). Then Facebook arrived with a superior product: the timeline. They didn't invent social networking, but they optimized it so effectively that billions of people migrated there.

## The Platform Architecture

Facebook's genius wasn't the technology—it was the business model. By providing free hosting, free tools, and reach to your audience, they made the value proposition irresistible. Why build your own website when you could reach more people on Facebook?

But this convenience came with a cost: Facebook owned the relationship with your audience. You could create content, but you couldn't own the platform it lived on. Their algorithm decided who saw your content. Their policies determined what was allowed. Their terms of service changed unilaterally.

## The Creator Economy Illusion

TikTok, YouTube, Instagram, Twitch—each presented itself as a platform for creators. And in a sense, they were. Millions of people built audiences. Some made serious money.

But the fundamental dynamic remained: the platform controlled the rules. Algorithms could change overnight, rendering your strategy obsolete. Monetization could be turned off. Accounts could be suspended. You were always renting, never owning.

## The Web3 Alternative (and its Problems)

Sensing this centralization, crypto evangelists promoted decentralized alternatives. Build on the blockchain. Own your data. No gatekeepers.

The technology had merit, but the execution was disastrous:
- High technical barriers to entry
- Speculative bubbles that distracted from utility
- Environmental concerns about energy use
- Concentration in the hands of venture capitalists (the new gatekeepers)

## The Forgotten Third Way: RSS and Federation

Before we got trapped in the walled gardens, there was another path. RSS feeds and blogging represented a middle ground:
- Decentralized hosting (your own server)
- Standardized distribution (RSS)
- User control over aggregation (RSS readers)

This approach lost not because it was technically inferior, but because it required users to take initiative. You had to choose what to read. No algorithm surfaced content automatically.

Interestingly, Mastodon (a decentralized Twitter alternative) is rediscovering these principles with federation—the ability to run your own server while interoperating with others.

## Why This Matters

The current platform architecture has consequences:

**For Users**
- Addictive by design
- Surveillance capitalism
- Limited control over data and content
- Filter bubbles and polarization

**For Society**
- A few companies control the infrastructure of discourse
- Centralized moderation of speech
- Vulnerability to coordinated outages
- Reduced privacy

**For Innovation**
- New creators must play by platform rules
- Alternative services struggle against network effects
- The next great idea might never be discovered because algorithms don't promote it

## A Possible Future

The path forward likely involves multiple competing models:
- Decentralized platforms with better UX
- Community-owned social networks
- Interoperable standards that reduce lock-in
- Greater regulation of platform gatekeepers
- A cultural shift back toward owning our digital presence

The open web isn't dead yet. But rebuilding it will require active choice, better tools, and a genuine commitment to decentralization over convenience.

The question isn't whether it's possible. It's whether we collectively want it enough to build it.
